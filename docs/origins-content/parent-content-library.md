# OFFENSE 1: Physical Fighting / Aggression
*TEC §37.006(a)(2) — Assault causing bodily injury*

---

## 🎬 VIDEO CONCEPT

**Title:** *"What To Do When Your Teen Says 'I Had To Fight'*"
**Hook (first 5 sec):** "Your kid comes home and says, 'I didn't start it.' Here's what to say next."
**Run time:** 3–4 min (YouTube) / 60 sec (Reels/TikTok)
**Format:** Parent-to-camera, warm living room setting

**Key Talking Points:**
1. Fighting rarely starts in the hallway — it usually starts in a text thread, a lunch table moment, or a feeling your kid couldn't name
2. Your job isn't to take sides — it's to help them understand what happened *inside* before the punch
3. The "rewind" conversation: ask them to walk you backward from the moment — what were they feeling 30 minutes before?
4. Teach the pause: "Is this worth my future?" — a single question that works for teens
5. Practice what "walking away" actually looks, sounds, and feels like — it's a skill, not a weakness

**Call to Action:** "Try the rewind conversation tonight — even if there was no fight. You're building a muscle before it's needed."

## 🏠 PROACTIVE HOME SUPPORTS

1. **Rewind Conversations** — Regularly ask your teen to "rewind" a tense moment from their week. Practice identifying the feeling, the trigger, and what they could do differently. Make this a habit at dinner or in the car — not just when something goes wrong.

2. **Name the Physical Signals** — Help your teen recognize *their own* anger signals: jaw tightening, heart racing, fists clenching. When they can name it, they can pause it. Ask: "What does your body feel like right before you get really mad?"

3. **Role-Play Walking Away** — Literally practice it. Stand up, turn, walk three steps. Talk about what to say: "I'm not doing this right now." Help them see it as confident, not cowardly.

4. **Create a Trusted Adult Map** — Work with your teen to identify two or three adults at school they can go to when tension is rising. Name them together. Make sure your teen knows using those adults is smart, not weak.

5. **Debrief Conflicts Early** — If your teen comes home frustrated about a peer situation, don't wait for it to escalate. Ask open questions: "How are things with [name]?" before it becomes a hallway confrontation.

6. **Celebrate De-escalation** — When your teen *chooses* to walk away from drama, name it and celebrate it. "I heard you didn't engage with that situation today. That takes real strength."

---

## 💡 HOW-TO TIPS

**How to start the rewind conversation:**
> "I'm not upset — I just want to understand. Walk me back from the moment. What happened an hour before?"

**How to talk about walking away without making it feel like weakness:**
> "Walking away doesn't mean they won. It means *you* chose your future over 30 seconds of feeling right."

**How to respond if your teen says 'I had to defend myself':**
> "I believe you felt like you had no choice. Let's talk about what we can set up so that next time, you have more choices."

## 🏠 PROACTIVE HOME SUPPORTS

1. **Regular Phone Check-Ins (Not Surveillance)** — Set a weekly time to casually scroll together. Not to police — to understand. "Show me something funny you saw this week" opens more than "let me see your messages."

2. **Empathy Practice at Home** — Read news stories or watch short videos together about people being treated unfairly. Ask: "How do you think that person felt?" Build the empathy muscle before it's tested.

3. **Talk About the Bystander Role** — Most teens aren't the bully or the target — they're bystanders. Talk about what it means to be an upstander: "What could you do if you saw someone being left out or mocked online?"

4. **Establish No-Shame Reporting** — Make it clear that telling an adult about bullying — of themselves or others — is never snitching. It's being someone who protects people.

5. **Model Respectful Disagreement** — How adults in the home talk about other people matters enormously. If your teen hears you mocking, gossiping, or putting others down, it normalizes that behavior.

---

## 💡 HOW-TO TIPS

**How to open the conversation without it feeling like an interrogation:**
> "I'm not checking up on you — I just want to understand your world. What's the social drama at school these days?"

**How to respond if your teen is being bullied:**
> "I'm so glad you told me. We're going to figure this out together. You don't have to handle this alone."

**How to respond if your teen has been bullying someone:**
> "I'm not angry at you — I'm worried about you. People who hurt others are usually hurting somewhere too. Let's talk about what's really going on."

## 🏠 PROACTIVE HOME SUPPORTS

1. **"Make Your Case" Practice** — Let your teen regularly make arguments for what they want — more screen time, a later curfew, skipping a chore. The rule: they must use calm tone, clear reasoning, and no interrupting. You coach the HOW, regardless of whether you say yes.

2. **De-brief School Frustrations** — When your teen vents about a teacher or rule, listen fully first. Then ask: "Is there a way you could bring that up to them that might actually change it?" Help them see advocacy as a tool.

3. **Teach the Pause Before Response** — Practice the "3-second rule": before responding to something that feels unfair, take three seconds. Model this yourself. Name it when you do it: "I'm taking three seconds before I respond to that."

4. **Role-Play Teacher Interactions** — Literally play the teacher. Give your teen a frustrating scenario ("I said your work isn't good enough") and coach them on how to respond respectfully: "Can you help me understand what I need to improve?"

5. **Help Them Understand Context** — Sometimes a teacher is wrong, stressed, or unfair. Help your teen understand: "Even when someone is wrong, there's a smart way and a costly way to respond."

---

## 💡 HOW-TO TIPS

**How to coach tone without dismissing feelings:**
> "I believe you that it felt unfair. Let's figure out how to talk about it in a way that the teacher will actually hear."

**How to respond when the school says your child was disruptive:**
> "Thank you for letting me know. Can you tell me more about what you observed? I want to understand it from your side too."

# OFFENSE 4: Drug, Alcohol & Vaping
*TEC §37.006(a)(4) — Drug/alcohol/tobacco/e-cigarette — Mandatory DAEP*

## 🏠 PROACTIVE HOME SUPPORTS

1. **Be the Excuse** — Explicitly give your teen permission to blame you in pressure situations. Rehearse it together: "My parents drug test me randomly." This removes the social cost of saying no.

2. **Talk About Belonging, Not Just Substances** — Most substance use at this age is about fitting in. Talk about peer pressure as a social pressure problem, not just a "good choice vs. bad choice" problem.

3. **Check In About Stress Levels Regularly** — Substance use often escalates when teens are overwhelmed. Create a regular check-in ritual: "On a scale of 1–10, how stressed are you this week?" and actually respond to a high number.

4. **Know Their People** — Know the names, parents, and general vibe of who your teen is spending time with. Not surveillance — just involvement. "I'd love to meet [friend] sometime." Connected parents are protective.

5. **Reduce Shame, Increase Safety** — Make it clear: "If you ever get into a situation that's unsafe, you can call me with no questions asked. Getting you home safe is always more important than being right." A teen who is afraid of disappointment will hide; a teen who trusts you will call.

6. **Talk About School Consequences Concretely** — Make sure your teen understands that drug/vaping violations are mandatory DAEP placements — not a choice the school makes. "I want you to know what happens, because I don't want that for you."

---

## 💡 HOW-TO TIPS

**How to respond if your teen admits they've tried something:**
> "Thank you for trusting me with that. I'm not going to flip out. I want to understand what was going on for you."

# OFFENSE 5: Threatening Language or Behavior

---

## 🏠 PROACTIVE HOME SUPPORTS

1. **Create Daily Emotional Check-Ins** — Not "how was school?" (they'll say fine). Try: "What was the hardest part of your day?" or "When did you feel most frustrated today?" Small daily moments build the relationship where they'll come to you with big feelings.

2. **Know the Warning Signs** — Increased isolation, giving possessions away, sudden calmness after being very upset, expressions of hopelessness ("nothing matters," "I won't have to deal with this much longer"). If you see these, reach out to a counselor immediately.

3. **Talk About Anger as Normal, Expression as a Choice** — "Feeling like you want to hurt someone is a normal feeling when you're really angry. Acting on it — or saying it in a way that scares people — that's where we need to be careful. Let's talk about how to get that anger out."

4. **Keep the Lines Open After a Scare** — If your teen said something threatening (even as venting), follow up the next day: "I've been thinking about what you said. How are you feeling today?" Don't let it be a one-time conversation.

5. **Normalize Professional Support** — Make therapy or counseling a regular topic — not a crisis response. "A lot of people I respect go to therapy. It's a smart way to get ahead of hard stuff."

---

## 💡 HOW-TO TIPS

**How to ask the direct question:**
> "I heard you say [X]. I'm not mad — I just want to make sure you're okay. Were you venting, or is something really wrong?"

**How to connect with the campus:**
> Call the counselor proactively: "My teen has been under a lot of stress and said something that concerned me. I wanted you to know so we're both paying attention."

## 🏠 PROACTIVE HOME SUPPORTS

1. **Have the Restitution Conversation** — If your teen has taken or damaged something, help them understand that making it right is more powerful than punishment alone. Walk through what "making it right" actually looks like: apologizing, paying back, repairing.

2. **Talk About Status and Peer Pressure Regularly** — Many teens steal to impress peers or because they feel "left behind" materially. Open conversations: "I know there's a lot of pressure to have certain things. Let's talk about that."

3. **Build the 'Ask Me' Habit** — Make it clear and safe for your teen to ask you for things — even things you might say no to. A teen who can ask has less reason to take.

4. **Connect Choices to Future** — Help teens understand the very real legal and school consequences of theft/vandalism in concrete terms: "A felony on your record at 16 doesn't disappear. Let's talk about what that actually means."

5. **Celebrate Integrity Moments** — When your teen is honest, returns something, admits a mistake — name it. "That took guts. I'm really proud of you for that."

---

## 💡 HOW-TO TIPS

**How to have the accountability conversation:**

**How to involve the school constructively:**
> "We've talked about what happened. My teen understands the seriousness. We want to make this right — what would restitution look like from the school's perspective?"

## 🏠 PROACTIVE HOME SUPPORTS

1. **Phone-Free Zones at Home** — Designate consistent phone-free times: meals, the first 30 min after school, the hour before bed. Frame it as wellness, not punishment: "Our brains need breaks from screens to actually work well."

2. **Phone Charging Stations Outside Bedrooms** — Phones charging in the kitchen at night removes the temptation entirely and models healthy limits. "It's not that I don't trust you — it's that none of us should have our phones by our beds."

5. **Debrief Phone Confiscations Without Shame** — If a teacher took the phone, use it as a coaching moment: "What was happening right before you took it out?" Help them identify the trigger.

## 🏠 PROACTIVE HOME SUPPORTS

1. **Investigate Before Escalating** — Before consequences, ask: "Is something happening at school that's making it hard to be there?" Anxiety, bullying, a social situation, or academic overwhelm are often the real cause.

2. **Build Predictable Morning Routines** — Most school refusal spikes in the morning. A calm, predictable morning reduces the daily negotiation. Sleep time, wake time, prep sequence — predictability is protective.

3. **Connect with the Counselor Early** — Don't wait for the attendance office to contact you. Reach out proactively: "My teen has been struggling to get to school. Can we talk about what support is available?"

4. **Small Re-Entry Plans** — If your teen has been out, returning to a full day can feel impossible. Work with the campus on partial days, a safe check-in person, or a modified schedule to rebuild the habit.

5. **Separate School from Worth** — Some teens check out because they feel like they're failing and don't see a way back. Remind them: "You haven't lost your chance. We just need to figure out the next step, not the whole staircase."

---

## 💡 HOW-TO TIPS

**How to respond to "I can't go today":**
> "I hear you. Can you tell me what's making it feel impossible right now? I'm not going to argue — I want to understand."

**How to work with the school on attendance:**
> "I know the attendance record looks bad. I want you to know we're aware of it and working on it. Can we put a support plan together?"
